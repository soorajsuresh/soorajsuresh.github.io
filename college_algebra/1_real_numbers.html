1.1 Distributive Property

1.1.1 What is The Distributive Property?

The Distributive Property (of multiplication of real numbers over addition of real numbers) states that any three real numbers $x$, $y$, and $z$ satisfy $x(y+z) =xy+xz$. Additionally, $(x+y)z =xz+yz$.

1.1.2 Applying The Distributive Property

We will simplify real expressions by applying The Distributive Property.

Example Let $a$ be a real number. Simplify $3 (a+4)+8$. Observe, $3(a+4)+8=3a+3\cdot4+8=3a+12+8=3a+20$.

We must be careful around minus signs.

Example Let $a$ and $b$ be real numbers. Expand $5 (a-b)-1$. Writing $5(a-b)=5a+5-b$ is a common mistake which causes serious problems down the road. When we distribute the $5$, it should be multiplied to each the $a$ and $-b$. But, when we write $5-b$, we are expressing subtraction, not multiplication. We remedy the situation using parentheses. Observe, $5(a-b)-1=5a+5(-b)-1=5a-5b-1$.

We use the second distributive property.

Example Let $a$ be a real number. Simplify $5+(a+8)3$. Observe: $5+(a+8)3=5+a3+8\cdot3=3a+5+24=3a+29$.

In the above example, we used commutativity of multiplication of real numbers to rewrite $a3$ as $3a$ and commutativity of addition of real numbers to reorganize the sum $5+3a$ as $3a+5$. The following is another example of using commutativity.

Example Let $a$ be a real number. Expand $4(a+5)3$. Observe: $4(a+5)3=4\cdot3(a+5)=12(a+5)=12a+12\cdot5=12a+60$. Alternatively, we could have started with distributivity, but we must be careful.
Writing $4(a+5)3=4a+4\cdot5\cdot3$ is a common mistake which causes serious problems down the road. The $3$ was to be multiplied to both terms in the sum $a+5$, but here it is only multiplying one of the terms in the sum $4a+4\cdot5$. We remedy the situation using parentheses. Observe: $4(a+5)3=(4a+4\cdot5)3=4a3+20\cdot3=4\cdot3a+60=12a+60$.

We increase the level of sophistication.

Example Let $a$ and $b$ be real numbers. Simplify $(3+a)(b+4)-3$. Observe: \begin{align}(3+a)(b+4)-3&=(3+a)b+(3+a)4-3\\ &=3b+ab+3\cdot4+a4-3\\ &=4a+3b+ab+12-3\\ &=4a+3b+ab+9\end{align}

Again.

Example Let $a$ and $b$ be real numbers. Simplify $3(a+2)(b-9)+11$. Observe: \begin{align}3(a+2)(b-9)+11&=3((a+2)b+(a+2)(-9))+11\\ &=3(ab+2b+a(-9)+2(-9))+11\\ &=3ab+3\cdot2b+3(-9a)+3(-18)+11\\ &=3ab+6b-27a-54+11\\ &=-27a+6b+3ab-43\end{align}

1.2 Negatives

1.2.1 Definition

The negative of a real number $x$ is the number $-x$ such that $x+-x=0$.

1.2.2 Properties

Let $x$ and $y$ be real numbers.

$-(-x)=x$ $-x=-1\cdot x$ $-(x+y)=-x+-y$ $-x\cdot y=x\cdot-y=-(x\cdot y)$ $-x\cdot -y=x\cdot y$ Let $y\ne0$. $\frac{-x}{-y}=\frac xy$ $\frac{-x}y=\frac x{-y}=-\frac xy$

1.2.3 Examples

Using the properties above, we will simplify negatives.

Example Let $a$ be a real number. Simplify $2- (5+a)$. Observe, $2-(5+a)=2-5-a=-3-a$.

We increase the level of sophistication.

Example Let $u$ and $v$ be real numbers. Simplify $u- (3-v)-4$. Observe, $u-(3-v)-4=u-3-(-v)-4=u-3+v-4=u+v-3-4=u+v-7$.

1.3 Fractions

1.3.1 Definitions

Let $x$ and $y$ are real numbers with $y\ne0$. It is convenient to use the notation $\frac xy:=xy^{-1}$. The object on the left is called a fraction. In this notation, $x$ is called the numerator of the fraction and $y$ the denominator of the fraction.

1.3.2 Properties of Fractions

Let $a$, $b$, $c$, and $d$ be real numbers with $b,d\ne0$. $x=\frac x1$ $\frac ab+\frac cd=\frac {ad+bc}{bd}$ $\frac ab-\frac cd=\frac {ad-bc}{bd}$ $\frac ab\cdot\frac cd=\frac {a\cdot c}{b\cdot d}$ $\frac ab=\frac {a\cdot d}{b\cdot d}$ If $c\ne0$, then $\left({\frac ab}\right)/\left({\frac cd}\right)=\frac{ad}{bc}$
