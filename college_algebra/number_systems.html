1.1 Natural Numbers

1.1.1 What are Natural Numbers?

The Natural Numbers is the set $\{0,\,1,\,2,\,\dots\}$ of the numbers $0$, $1$, $2$, $\dots$.

1.1.2 Notation

We can use the notation $\mathbb N:=\{0,\,1,\,2,\,\dots\}$. On paper, I draw two vertical bars and then complete a capital N starting from the leftmost vertical bar.

1.1.3 Division Algorithm

If $n$ and $q>0$ are natural numbers, then there are unique natural numbers $m$ and $r$ such that $n=mq+r$ and $0\le r<q$.

For example, let $n=7$. We will look at $q\in\{1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8\}.$

1.1.4 Prime and Composite Numbers

A natural number $n>1$ is called a prime number if its only divisors are $1$ and $n$.

A natural number $n>1$ is called a composite number if it has divisors other than $1$ and $n$.

Is the number $1$ prime, composite, or neither? Is the number $0$ prime, composite, or neither? Can a number be both prime and composite?

1.1.5 The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Any natural number can be written as a unique product of prime numbers.

Why do you think $n>1$ is stipulated in 1.1.4?

1.2 Integers

1.2.1 Negatives of Natural Numbers

The negative of a natural number $n$ is the number $-n$ such that $n+-n=0$.

1.2.2 What are Integers?

The Integers is the set of all natural numbers along with their negatives.

Is any integer its own negative?

1.2.3 Notation

We can use the notation $\mathbb Z:=\{\dots,\,-2,\,-1,\,0,\,1,\,2,\,\dots\}$. On paper, I draw a capital Z and add an extra diagonal bar from the top middle to the bottom left. But now I am considering drawing the first two strokes (top and diagonal) of the Z and then drawing the last two strokes (diagonal and bottom) offset to the left. The Z comes from the German word "zahlen" for "numbers".

1.2.4 Even and Odd Integers

An even integer is an integer of the form $2z$ where $z\in\mathbb Z$.

An odd integer is an integer of the form $2z+1$ where $z\in\mathbb Z$.

We can use the notation $2\mathbb Z=\{2z\mid z\in\mathbb Z\}=\{\dots,\,-4,\,-2,\,0,\,2,\,4\}$ for the set of all even integers and $2\mathbb Z+1:=\{2z+1\mid z\in\mathbb Z\}=\{\dots,\,-3,\,-1,\,1,\,3,\,\dots\}$ for the set of all odd integers.

What is the most odd prime number?

1.3 Rational Numbers

1.3.1 Reciprocals of Integers

The reciprocal of a nonzero integer $z$ is the number $\frac1z$ such that $z\cdot\frac1z=1$.

Is there an integer without a reciprocal?

1.3.2 What are Rational Numbers?

The Rational Numbers is the set of all products of integers and their reciprocals.

1.3.3 Notation

We can use the notation $\mathbb Q:=\{pq^{-1}\mid p,q\in\mathbb Z, q\ne0\}$.

1.5 Irrational Numbers

1.5.1 What are Irrational Numbers?

The Irrational Numbers is the set of all real numbers that are not rational numbers.

1.5.2 Notation

We can use the suggestive notation $\mathbb R-\mathbb Q$.

1.5 Complex Numbers

1.5.1 The Imaginary Unit

The imaginary unit is $\sqrt{-1}$.

For convenience, we can use the notation $\mathrm i:=\sqrt{-1}$

1.5.2 Imaginary Numbers

An imaginary number is the product of the imaginary unit with a real number.

1.5.3 What are Complex Numbers

The Complex Numbers is the set of all sums of real numbers and imaginary numbers.

1.5.4 Notation

We can use the notation $\mathbb C:=\{a+\mathrm{i}b\mid a,b\in\mathbb R\}$. On paper, I draw a vertical bar inside a capital C.
