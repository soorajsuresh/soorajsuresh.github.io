Mean Value Theorem

1.1 Rolle's Theorem

1.1.1 What is Rolle's Theorem?

If $f$ is a function continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$ such that $f(a)=f(b)$, then there exists a $c\in(a,b)$ such that $f'(c)=0$.

1.1.2 Verifying Rolle's Theorem

By Rolle's Theorem, the existence of such a $c$ is guaranteed, but that does not mean we will be able to find it. Let us look at some contrived examples in which we are able to find $c$.

Example Let $f$ be the function defined by $f(x)=x(x-1)^2$ on $[0,1]$. Determine whether the hypotheses of Rolle's Theorem are met. If they are, find $c$ guaranteed by the theorem.

Note, $f$ is clearly continuous on $[0,1]$ and differentiable on $(0,1)$.

Indeed, by properties of continuous functions, $x$ and $x-1$ are continuous on $[0,1]$, and the power $(x-1)^2$ of a function that is continuous on $[0,1]$ is also continuous on $[0,1]$, and the product $x(x-1)^2$ of functions that are continuous on $[0,1]$ is continuous on $[0,1]$.

By properties of differentiable functions, $x$ and $x-1$ are differentiable on $(0,1)$, and the power $(x-1)^2$ of a function that is differentiable on $(0,1)$ is also differentiable on $(0,1)$, and the product $x(x-1)^2$ of functions that are differentiable on $(0,1)$ is differentiable on $(0,1)$.

Note, $f(0)=0=f(1)$.

So, the hypotheses of Rolle's Theorem are met. Therefore, by Rolle's Theorem, there exists a $c\in(0,1)$ such that $f'(c)=0$.

Note, $f(x)=x(x-1)^2=x(x^2-2x+1)=x^3-2x^2+x$, so $f'(x)=3x^2-4x+1$, so $f'(c)=3c^2-4c+1=0$, so $(3c-1)(c-1)=0$, so $3c-1=0$ or $c-1=0$, so $c=\frac13$ or $c=1$.

Note $c=1\notin(0,1)$ and $c=1/3\in(0,1)$. So, the $c$ we seek is $c=1/3$.

1.2 The Mean Value Theorem

1.2.1 What is The Mean Value Theorem?

If $f$ is a function continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then there exists a $c\in(a,b)$ such that $f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$.

1.2.2 Verifying The Mean Value Theorem

Let us look at some contrived examples in which we are able to find $c$.

Example Let $f$ be the function defined by $f(x)=x^3-3x+2$ on $[-2,2]$. Determine whether the hypotheses of the Mean Value Theorem are met. If they are, find a $c$ that verifies the theorem.

Note, $f$ is clearly continuous on $[-2,2]$ and differentiable on $(-2,2)$.

Indeed, polynomials are continuous and differentiable everywhere.

So, the hypotheses of The Mean Value Theorem are met. Therefore, by The Mean Value Theorem, there exists a $c\in(-2,2)$ such that $f'(c)=\frac{f(2)-f(-2)}{2-(-2)}$.

Note, $f(2)=2^3-3(2)+2=4$ and $f(-2)=(-2)^3-3(-2)+2=0$, so $f'(c)=\frac{4-0}{2-(-2)}=1$.

Note, $f'(x)=3x^2-3$, so $f'(c)=3c^2-3=1$, so $3c^2=4$, so $c^2=\frac43$ so $c=\pm\frac2{\sqrt3}$.

Note $\pm\frac2{\sqrt3}\in(-2,2)$, so, either of $2/\sqrt3$ and $-2/\sqrt3$ can be chosen as the $c$ we seek.
