A vector field is a function $\F$ that assigns a vector to each point in its domain. For example, for a domain in $\mathbb{R}^3$,

$ \F(x,y,z) = P(x,y,z) \mathbf{i} + Q(x,y,z) \mathbf{j} + R(x,y,z) \mathbf{k} $

A vector field is continuous at a point when its component functions are each continuous at that point, continuous when its component functions are each continuous, and differentiable when its component functions are each differentiable.

We have seen vector fields before: $\T(t)$, $\N(t)$, and $\B(t)$, for example. Even $\r(t)$ and $\v(t)$ can be called vector fields.

Recall, the gradient vector $\nabla f$ of a differentiable real-valued function $f$ at a point $(x,y,z)$ gives the direction of greatest increase. The gradient field of $f(x,y,z)$ is

$ \nabla f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\mathbf{k} $

that assigns to every point of $f$ a vector pointing in the direction of greatest increase.

If the temperature $T$ of a material at each point $(x,y,z)$ is $T(x,y,z) = 100 - x^2 - y^2 - z^2$ and $\F$ is its gradient field, find $\F$.

Let

$ \F(x,y,z) = P(x,y,z) \mathbf{i} + Q(x,y,z) \mathbf{j} + R(x,y,z) \mathbf{k} $
be a continuous vector field, and let $C$ be a smooth curve parametrized by
$ \mathbf{r}(t) = a(t) \mathbf{i} + b(t) \mathbf{j} + c(t) \mathbf{k}, \quad \alpha \le t \le \beta $

The direction along $C$ defined by the parametrization is called the forward direction.

Note, $\T$ (the unit tangent vector) points in the forward direction. The scalar tangential component of $\F$ along $C$ is

$ \F \cdot \T = \F \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{ds} $

If $\F$ is a continuous vector field defined on a smooth curve $C$ parametrized by $\mathbf{r}(t)$, then the line integral of $\F$ along $C$ is
$ \int_C \F \cdot \T \, ds $

Note,

$ \int_C \F \cdot \T \, ds = \int_C (\F \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{ds}) ds = \int_C \F \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_\alpha^\beta \F(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) dt $

Find $\int_C \F \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ for $\F(x,y,z) = \langle z, xy, -y^2 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle t^2, t, \sqrt{t} \rangle$, $0 \le t \le 1$.
Define $\int_C P(x,y,z) dx$, $\int_C Q(x,y,z) dy$, and $\int_C R(x,y,z) dz$.

The sum of line integrals

$ \int_C P dx + \int_C Q dy + \int_C R dz $
is denoted
$ \int_C P dx + Q dy + R dz $

Evaluate $\int_C -y dx + z dy + 2x dz$ where $C$ is the helix $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle \cos t, \sin t, t \rangle$, $0 \le t \le 2\pi$.

If the vector $\F(P)$ represents a force, $\F$ is called a force field. Let $C$ be smooth and $\F$ a continuous force field. Partition $C$ into $n$ arcs of lengths $\Delta s_k$ and points $(x_k, y_k, z_k)$ with tangent vectors $\T(x_k, y_k, z_k)$. Then the work along each arc is approximated by

$ W_k \approx \F(x_k, y_k, z_k) \cdot (\T(x_k, y_k, z_k) \Delta s_k) $
and total work
$ W = \sum_{k=1}^n W_k \approx \sum_{k=0}^n (\F(x_k, y_k, z_k) \cdot \T(x_k, y_k, z_k)) \Delta s_k = S_n $

For any partition, the limit $\lim_{n \to \infty} S_n$ exists and equals

$ \int_C \F \cdot \T \, ds $

If $C$ is smooth and $\F$ is a continuous force field, the work done along $C$ is
$ W = \int_C \F \cdot \T \, ds $
Find the work done by $\F = (x-y)\mathbf{i} + x \mathbf{j}$ along $\mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t)\mathbf{i} + (\sin t)\mathbf{j}$, $0 \le t \le 2\pi$.
If $C$ is smooth and $\F$ a continuous velocity field, the flow along $C$ is $\int_C \F \cdot \T \, ds$. If $C$ starts and ends at the same point, this flow is called circulation.
Find the circulation of $\F = (x-y)\mathbf{i} + x \mathbf{j}$ around $\mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t)\mathbf{i} + (\sin t)\mathbf{j}$, $0 \le t \le 2\pi$.
A curve in the plane is simple if it does not cross itself.
A curve that starts and ends at the same point is a closed curve or loop.

When $C$ is a closed curve, the line integral $\int_C f(x,y,z) ds$ is denoted $\oint_C f(x,y,z) ds$. Traversed counterclockwise, it is denoted $\ointctrclockwise_C f(x,y,z) ds$.

If $C$ is a smooth simple closed plane curve, $\F$ continuous over a region containing $C$, and $\n$ the outward unit normal vector on $C$, the flux of $\F$ across $C$ is
$ \int_C \F \cdot \n \, ds $

Let $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle a(t), b(t) \rangle$, $\alpha \le t \le \beta$ parameterize $C$. If traversed clockwise (CW), $\n = \mathbf{k} \times \T$. If counterclockwise (CCW), $\n = \T \times \mathbf{k}$.

For CCW traversal:

$ \n = \T \times \mathbf{k} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \frac{dx}{ds} & \frac{dy}{ds} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \frac{dy}{ds} \mathbf{i} - \frac{dx}{ds} \mathbf{j} $

If $\F = \langle P(x,y), Q(x,y) \rangle$, then

$ \F \cdot \n = P(x,y) \frac{dy}{ds} - Q(x,y) \frac{dx}{ds} $
so
$ \int_C \F \cdot \n \, ds = \ointctrclockwise_C (P \, dy - Q \, dx) $

Find the flux of $\F = \langle x-y, x \rangle$ across the unit circle.
