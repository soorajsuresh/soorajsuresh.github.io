Recall,

\[ \curl \mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \langle \partial_x, \partial_y, \partial_z \rangle \times \langle P, Q, R \rangle = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z \\ P & Q & R \end{vmatrix} = (R_y - Q_z)\mathbf{i} + (P_z - R_x)\mathbf{j} + (Q_x - P_y)\mathbf{k}. \]

The direction of $\curl \mathbf{F}$ represents the axis around which particles in the fluid $\mathbf{F}$ rotate, and the magnitude of $\curl \mathbf{F}$ represents the rate of the rotation.

Stokes' Theorem (The Curl Theorem)
If $S$ is a piecewise smooth surface oriented by $\mathbf{n}$ with a piecewise smooth boundary curve $C$ parametrized by $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{F}$ is a vector field whose components have continuous first partial derivatives on an open region containing $S$, then
$\displaystyle \ointctrclockwise_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S \curl \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, d\sigma$

By Stokes' Theorem, if two different surfaces $S_1$ and $S_2$ oriented by $\mathbf{n}_1$ and $\mathbf{n}_2$ have the same boundary $C$, then

$\iint_{S_1} \curl \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n}_1 \, d\sigma = \iint_{S_2} \curl \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n}_2 \, d\sigma$.
This also means that to find $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $C$ is the curve of intersection between $S_1$ and $S_2$, either
$\iint_{S_1} \curl \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n}_1 \, d\sigma$
or
$\iint_{S_2} \curl \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n}_2 \, d\sigma$
can be computed instead.

Note, $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{k}$ yields the circulation-curl form of Green's Theorem.

Evaluate $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = -y^2 \mathbf{i} + x \mathbf{j} + z^2 \mathbf{k}$ and $C$ is the curve of intersection of the plane $y+z=2$ and the cylinder $x^2+y^2=1$.
