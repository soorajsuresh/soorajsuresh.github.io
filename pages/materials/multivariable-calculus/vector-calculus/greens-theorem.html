Let $\F(x,y)=P(x,y)\mathbf{i}+Q(x,y)\mathbf{j}$ be a velocity field of a fluid flowing in the plane where $P$ and $Q$ have continuous first partials on a region $E$. Let $(x,y)$ be a point in $E$, and let $R$ be a small rectangle with one corner at $(x,y)$ with sides parallel to the $x$- and $y$-axes of length $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ such that $R$ and its interior are contained in $E$.

The circulation rate of a velocity field along a curve is the tangential component of the velocity field along the curve. The circulation rate of $\F$ along $R$ is the sum of the circulation rates of $\F$ along each side of $R$.

Top:$\F(x, y+\Delta y)\cdot(-\mathbf{i}\Delta x)=-P(x,y+\Delta y)\Delta x$
Bottom:$\F(x, y)\cdot(\mathbf{i}\Delta x)=P(x,y)\Delta x$
Right:$\F(x+\Delta x, y)\cdot(\mathbf{j}\Delta y)=Q(x+\Delta x,y)\Delta y$
Left:$\F(x, y)\cdot(-\mathbf{j}\Delta y)=-Q(x,y)\Delta y$

Sum of top and bottom:

$ -P(x,y+\Delta y)\Delta x + P(x,y)\Delta x \approx -(\partial_y P)\Delta x \Delta y $

Sum of right and left:

$ Q(x+\Delta x,y)\Delta y - Q(x,y)\Delta y \approx (\partial_x Q)\Delta x \Delta y $

So, circulation rate per unit area (circulation density) is

$ \partial_x Q - \partial_y P $

The circulation density of a vector field $\F=P\mathbf{i}+Q\mathbf{j}$ at the point $(x,y)$ is
$ \partial_x Q - \partial_y P $

So, for $\F = P\mathbf{i} + Q\mathbf{j}$, the circulation density is the $\mathbf{k}$-component of the curl: $\mathrm{curl}\,\F \cdot \mathbf{k} = Q_x - P_y$. It measures the rate and direction of rotation of the fluid in the plane: positive means counterclockwise rotation.

The flux of a velocity field along a curve is the normal component across the curve. The flux of $\F$ across $R$ is the sum of the fluxes along each side:

Top:$\F(x, y+\Delta y)\cdot(\mathbf{j}\Delta x)=Q(x,y+\Delta y)\Delta x$
Bottom:$\F(x, y)\cdot(-\mathbf{j}\Delta x)=-Q(x,y)\Delta x$
Right:$\F(x+\Delta x, y)\cdot(\mathbf{i}\Delta y)=P(x+\Delta x,y)\Delta y$
Left:$\F(x, y)\cdot(-\mathbf{i}\Delta y)=-P(x,y)\Delta y$

Flux density per unit area:

$ \partial_x P + \partial_y Q $

The divergence (flux density) of $\F=P\mathbf{i}+Q\mathbf{j}$ at $(x,y)$ is
$ \mathrm{div}\,\F = \partial_x P + \partial_y Q $

So, $\mathrm{div}\,\F$ measures the local expansion or compression of a fluid: positive divergence indicates flow spreading out.

A simple closed curve $C$ is positively oriented if the region it encloses is always to the left as one moves along $C$ counterclockwise, and negatively oriented if the region is to the right when traversed clockwise.

Line integral along a positively oriented curve:

$ \oint_{\mathrm{CCW}} \F \cdot \T ds $

Reversing orientation reverses the sign of the line integral.

Green's Theorem (Circulation-Curl Form or Tangential Form)
If $C$ is a piecewise smooth, simple closed curve enclosing a region $R$ in the plane and $\F=P\mathbf{i}+Q\mathbf{j}$ has continuous first partials in an open region containing $R$, then
$ \oint_{\mathrm{CCW}} \F \cdot \T ds = \iint_R (\mathrm{curl}\,\F \cdot \mathbf{k}) dA $

Recall:

$ \oint_{\mathrm{CCW}} \F \cdot \T ds = \oint_{\mathrm{CCW}} P dx + Q dy $
$ \iint_R (\mathrm{curl}\,\F \cdot \mathbf{k}) dA = \iint_R (Q_x - P_y) dA $

Green's Theorem (Flux-Divergence Form or Normal Form)
If $C$ is piecewise smooth, simple closed, enclosing $R$, and $\F=P\mathbf{i}+Q\mathbf{j}$ with continuous first partials, then
$ \oint_{\mathrm{CCW}} \F \cdot \mathbf{n} ds = \iint_R (\mathrm{div}\,\F) dA $

Recall:

$ \oint_{\mathrm{CCW}} \F \cdot \mathbf{n} ds = \oint_{\mathrm{CCW}} P dy - Q dx $
$ \iint_R (\mathrm{div}\,\F) dA = \iint_R (P_x + Q_y) dA $

If $\F(x,y)=(x-y)\mathbf{i}+x\mathbf{j}$, determine the flow along $C$ and the flux across $C$, where $C$ is the unit circle.
Evaluate $\int_C (x-y) dx + (x+y) dy$, where $C$ is the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,3)$, and $(0,3)$.
Verify Green's Theorem for $\F(x,y) = (xy^2 + \arctan x)\mathbf{i} + (x^2 y - y \sinh y)\mathbf{j}$ and the region $R$ bounded by $C = C_1 \cup C_2 \cup C_3$, where $C_1$ is the line from $(0,0)$ to $(4,0)$, $C_2$ from $(4,0)$ to $(4,2)$, and $C_3$ is $y = \sqrt{x}$ from $(4,2)$ to $(0,0)$.
Use Green's Theorem to determine the area of an ellipse with semi-axes of lengths $a$ and $b$.
