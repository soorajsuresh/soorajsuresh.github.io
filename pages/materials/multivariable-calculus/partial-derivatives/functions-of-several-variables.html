Functions of Several Variables

Let $D$ be a set of $n$-tuples $(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ of real numbers (points in $\R^n$). A real-valued function $f$ on $D$ takes each $n$-tuple from $D$ as an input and pairs it with a real number output
$w=f(x_1,\dots,x_n).$
The set $D$ of inputs is called the domain of $f$ and the set of all outputs is called the range of $f$. In the equation above, $w$ is called a dependent variable and $x_1,\dots,x_n$ are called independent variables.

It is often the case that for a function $f$ of two independent variables, we call the independent variables $x$ and $y$ and the dependent variable $z$. The domain can be graphed in the $xy$-plane, and the function values form a surface in space.

$f(x,y)=\frac{xy^2+y^3}{\sqrt{x-2}\parens{y-\cosh x}}.$

In our past we have used the words open and closed to describe intervals on the number line. What makes an open interval open? What makes a closed interval closed?

Now we will extend the idea to the plane.

A disk of radius $r>0$ in the $xy$-plane is the set of all points $(x,y)$ satisfying $x^2+y^2<r$.
Let $R$ be a region in the plane. A point in $R$ is called an interior point of $R$ if it is the center of a disk that lies entirely in $R$. A point in the $xy$-plane is called a boundary point of $R$ if every disk centered at the point contains points inside and outside of $R$. The set of all interior points of $R$ is called the interior of $R$ and the set of all boundary points of $R$ is called the boundary of $R$. A region is called open if it consists entirely of interior points and closed if it contains all of its boundary points.
A region in the plane is called bounded if it is contained in a disk. If a region is not bounded, it is called unbounded.
State (without proof) whether or not the region $R$ in the plane is bounded or unbounded if
$R=\build{(x,y)\in\R^2}{\abs x<1\text,\ \abs y\le1}\text.$
Justify your conclusion with a sketch of the graph of $R$.
State (without proof) whether or not the region $R$ in the plane is bounded or unbounded if
$R=\build{(x,y)\in\R^2}{y=\tanh x\text{ or }y=\ln x}\text.$
Justify your conclusion with a sketch of the graph of $R$.

Let $f$ be a function with a domain in the plane. The set of all points $(x,y,f(x,y))$ in space is called the graph of $f$. The set of all points $(x,y)$ in the plane such that $f$ has a constant value $f(x,y)=c$ is called a level curve of $f$.
Let $f$ be defined by $f(x,y)=y^2-x^2$. Present a sketch of the graph of $f$, labeling all of its intercepts with the coordinate axes, as well as a sketch of the level curves given by $f(x,y)=-1$, $f(x,y)=0$, and $f(x,y)=1$.

The definitions of interior points, boundary points, open sets, closed sets, bounded, and unbounded in space are analogous to the definitions in the plane. Analogously to the intervals and disks considered in $\R^1$ and $\R^2$, respectively, we consider balls in $\R^3$. If $f$ is a function with a domain in space, then the set of all points $(x,y,z)$ in space such that $f$ has a constant value $f(x,y,z)=c$ is called a level surface of $f$.
