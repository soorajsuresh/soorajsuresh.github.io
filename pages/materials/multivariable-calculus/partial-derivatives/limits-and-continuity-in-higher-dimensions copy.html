Limits and Continuity in Higher Dimensions

In the plane, we said that $f(x)$ approaches the limit $L$ as $x$ approaches $a$ if $f(x)$ is close to $L$ for any value $x$ close enough to $a$. The value $x$ can approach $a$ from the right or left.

More precisely, the phrase

$f(x)$ approaches $L$ as $x$ approaches $a$
means that for every number $\epsilon>0$, there exists a corresponding number $\delta>0$ such that for every value $x$ in the domain of $f$,
|f(x)-L|<\epsilon \text{ whenever } 0<|x-a|<\delta.

We can say that $f(x,y)$ approaches $L$ as $(x,y)$ approaches $(x_0,y_0)$ if $f(x,y)$ is close to a fixed real number $L$ for any point $(x,y)$ close enough to a point $(x_0,y_0)$. The point $(x,y)$ can approach $(x_0,y_0)$ along any of infinitely many paths.

Suppose that every disk centered at $(x_0,y_0)$ contains a point in the domain of $f$ other than $(x_0,y_0)$ itself. The phrase
$f(x,y)$ approaches $L$ as $(x,y)$ approaches $(x_0,y_0)$
denoted by
$\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} f(x,y) = L$
means that for every number $\epsilon>0$, there exists a corresponding number $\delta>0$ such that for every $(x,y)$ in the domain of $f$,
|f(x,y)-L|<\epsilon \text{ whenever } 0<\sqrt{(x-x_0)^2+(y-y_0)^2}<\delta.
If $f(x,y)$ approaches $L$ as $(x,y)$ approaches $(x_0,y_0)$, then $L$ is called the limit of $f(x,y)$ as $(x,y)$ approaches $(x_0,y_0)$. Otherwise, the limit does not exist.
Prove by definition that
$\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \arctan\parens{\frac{3}{5}x^2+y^2}^4 = 0$.
Prove by definition that
$\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{9x^{11}y\sqrt[3]{y^7}}{\sqrt[5]{4x^2+y^2}} = 0$.
Properties of Limits of Functions of Two Variables:
Constant RuleFor any number $k$, $\lim\limits_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} k = k$.
Single Variable Function Rules$\lim\limits_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} g(x) = \lim\limits_{x\to x_0} g(x)$
$\lim\limits_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} h(y) = \lim\limits_{y\to y_0} h(y)$
Sum and Difference Rules$\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} [f(x,y) \pm g(x,y)] = L \pm M$
Constant Multiple Rule$\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} k f(x,y) = k L$
Product Rule$\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} [f(x,y) \cdot g(x,y)] = L \cdot M$
Quotient RuleIf $M \ne 0$, then $\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} \frac{f(x,y)}{g(x,y)} = \frac{L}{M}$
Composition RuleIf $h$ is continuous at $L$, then $\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} h(f(x,y)) = h(L)$
Determine
$\lim\limits_{(x,y)\to(0,3)} \left(\tan^3{5x} \csc^3{2x} + \frac{9(y-3)^{-2n}}{e^{\frac{2}{6-2y}}}\right)$.
Determine
$\lim\limits_{(x,y)\to(1,1)} \frac{x^5+2x^4y-6x^3y^2-4x^2y^3+13xy^4-6y^5}{(\sqrt[4]{x}-\sqrt[4]{y})(x-y)^2}$.
Test for Nonexistence of a Limit: If a function $f$ approaches different limits as $(x,y)$ approaches $(x_0,y_0)$ along different paths, then $\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} f(x,y)$ does not exist.
Show that the function
$f(x,y) = \frac{2x^2y}{x^4+y^2}$
has no limit as $(x,y)$ approaches $(0,0)$.
Suppose that every disk centered at $(x_0,y_0)$ contains a point in the domain of $f$ other than $(x_0,y_0)$ itself. Then $f(x,y)$ is continuous at the point $(x_0,y_0)$ if:
  1. $f$ is defined at $(x_0,y_0)$
  2. $\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} f(x,y)$ exists
  3. $\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} f(x,y) = f(x_0,y_0)$
If a function is not continuous at a point, it is called discontinuous at that point. A function is continuous if it is continuous at every point of its domain.
Algebraic Combinations of Continuous Functions are Continuous: Constant multiples, sums and differences, products, quotients, and powers of continuous functions of two variables are continuous at every point in their domain. In particular, polynomials and rational functions of two variables are continuous at every point in their domain.
Suppose $f$ is a real-valued function of two real variables and $g$ is a real-valued function of one real variable. If $f$ is continuous at $(x_0,y_0)$ and $g$ is continuous at $f(x_0,y_0)$, then the composition $h=g\circ f$ defined by $h(x,y)=g(f(x,y))$ is also continuous at $(x_0,y_0)$.
Prove that $f$ defined by
$ f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \sinh\left(\dfrac{9(x-5)^{11}(y+2)\sqrt[3]{(y+2)^7}}{\sqrt[5]{4(x-5)^2+(y+2)^2}}\right), & (x,y) \ne (5,-2) \\ 0, & (x,y)=(5,-2) \end{cases} $
is continuous.
Prove that $f$ defined by
$ f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{4(x+3)^{1/3}\sqrt{y}}{(x+3)^{2/3}+6y}, & (x,y) \ne (-3,0) \\ 0, & (x,y)=(-3,0) \end{cases} $
is discontinuous at $(x,y)=(-3,0)$.

The definitions and theorems of limits and continuity can straightforwardly be extended to functions of three or more variables.
