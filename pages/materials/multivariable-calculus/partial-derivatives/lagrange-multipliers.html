Lagrange Multipliers

Find the points on the hyperbolic cylinder $x^2 - z^2 - 1 = 0$ that are closest to the origin.

A clever approach is to consider a sphere centered at the origin with radius $a$, and increase $a$ until the sphere touches the hyperbolic cylinder.

At the points of intersection between the sphere and cylinder, both surfaces share the same tangent plane and normal line.

Represent the sphere and cylinder as level surfaces $f(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 - a^2 = 0$ and $g(x,y,z) = x^2 - z^2 - 1 = 0$. The gradients $\nabla f$ and $\nabla g$ are parallel at the points of intersection:

$\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g \quad \text{for some scalar } \lambda$
$\langle 2x, 2y, 2z \rangle = \lambda \langle 2x, 0, -2z \rangle$

Equating components:

$2x = 2\lambda x, \quad 2y = 0, \quad 2z = -2\lambda z$

Solving gives $y = 0$, $z = 0$, and $x = \pm 1$. So the points are $(\pm 1, 0, 0)$.

The Orthogonal Gradient Theorem
Let $f(x,y,z)$ be differentiable in a region containing a smooth curve $C$ parametrized by $\mathbf{r}(t)$. If $P_0$ is a point on $C$ at which $f$ has a local extremum along $C$, then
$\nabla f(P_0) \perp \mathbf{r}'(P_0)$.

Proof idea: By the chain rule, $\frac{df}{dt} = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{r}'$. At a local extremum along $C$, $\frac{df}{dt}|_{P_0} = 0$, so the gradient is orthogonal to the tangent vector.

This result holds for $f(x,y)$ as well.

The Method of Lagrange Multipliers
If $f$ has a local extremum subject to the constraint $g = 0$, then the extremum occurs at points $(x,y,z)$ satisfying $g(x,y,z) = 0$ such that
$\nabla f(x,y,z) = \lambda \nabla g(x,y,z)$
for some scalar $\lambda$.

To find absolute extrema of $f$ subject to $g=0$, compare the values of $f$ at all such points.
