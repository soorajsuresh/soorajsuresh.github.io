Vectors

[REWORK] A vector is a quantity that has both a magnitude and a direction. To distinguish vectors from numbers, vectors should be typeset in boldface ($\a$) and handwritten with an arrow or bar above them like $\overline{a}$ or $\vec{a}$. A vector is (represented by) an arrow. The arrow points in the direction of the vector and its length represents the magnitude of the vector. If the arrow starts at a point $A$ and ends at another point $B$, then we write
$\a=\overrightarrow{AB}$,
and $A$ is called the initial point or tail of the vector and $B$ is called the terminal point or tip or head of the vector.

[displacement] Let $A$, $B$, and $C$ be points in space. If a particle moves from $A$ to $B$ and then from $B$ to $C$, then, in total, it has moved from $A$ to $C$. If $\a=\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\b=\overrightarrow{BC}$, then $\c=\overrightarrow{AC}$ is called the resultant vector. The vector $\c$ is also called the sum of $\a$ and $\b$ denoted $\c =\a+\b$.

If two vectors have the same direction, they are said to be parallel.

If $\a$ is a vector, then $-\a$ is the vector with the same magnitude as $\a$ but opposite direction.

If $\a$ is a vector, then $2\a$ is a vector parallel to $\a$ that is twice as long as $\a$. In general, if $k$ is a number, then $k\a$ is a vector $k$ times as long as $\a$ in the same direction of $\a$ if $k>0$ and in the opposite direction if $k<0$. The number $k$ is called a scalar and the vector $k\a$, read as $k$ times $\a$, is called the scalar product of $k$ and $\a$ and is also called a scalar multiple of $\a$. Note, $-\a=-1\a$.

If $k\ne0$, then $\frac\a k$ denotes the vector parallel to $\a$ than which $\a$ is $k$ times longer

$\frac\a k=\frac1k\a$.

The difference of $\a$ and $\b$ is the vector $\a-\b=\a+(-\b)$.

Two vectors are equal if they have the same length and direction.

There are infinitely many vectors equal to $\overrightarrow{AB}$, each with a distinct initial point. Exactly one of these vectors has an intial point at the origin. This vector is said to be in \defn{standard position} and it is called the \defn{position vector} of $\overrightarrow{AB}$. Note, a position vector is completely and uniquely determined by its terminal point.

If a position vector $\a$ has a terminal point in space described in rectangular coordinates as $(a_1,a_2,a_3)$, then it is denoted \[\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}.\] This notation is called the \defn{component form} of $\a$ and the numbers $a_1$, $a_2$, and $a_3$ are called the \defn{components} of $\a$. The symbols $\langle$ and $\rangle$ are called angle brackets.

Two vectors $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ and $\b=\inner{b_1,b_2,b_3}$ are equal if and only if their corresponding components are equal, i.e., $\a=\b$ if and only if $a_1=b_1$, $a_2=b_2$, and $a_3=b_3$.

The magnitude (or length) of $\a$ is denoted by $\abs{\a}$.

If $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$, then $\abs{\a}=\sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2}$.

The \defn{zero vector}, denoted $\bm 0=\inner{0,0,0}$ has magnitude $0$ and has no direction.

If $P=(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ and $Q=(x_2,y_2,z_2)$, then
$\overrightarrow{PQ}=\inner{x_2-x_1,\,y_2-y_1,\,z_2-z_1}$,
and
$\abs{\overrightarrow{PQ}}=\sqrt{\parens{x_2-x_1}^2+\parens{y_2-y_1}^2+\parens{z_2-z_1}^2}$.
If $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ and $\b=\inner{b_1,b_2,b_3}$, then
$\a+\b=\inner{a_1+b_1,\,a_2+b_2,\,a_3+b_3}$.
If $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ and $k\in\R$, then
$k\a=\inner{ka_1,\,ka_2,\,ka_3}$.
The Parallelogram Law: If $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ and $\b=\inner{b_1,b_2,b_3}$ are vectors, then
$\a+\b=\b+\a$,
and the direction of $\a+\b$ points along the diagonal of the parallelogram determined by $\a$ and $\b$.
\begin{example} If $\bm{u}=\inner{2,-1}$, then find the magnitude of $-3\bm u$. \end{example} \begin{example} If $\bm u=\inner{4,-1,3}$ and $\bm v=\inner{-1,-1,0}$, then find the component form and magnitude of $2\bm u-3\bm v$. \end{example}
An airplane is traveling with a bearing of S$25^\circ$W (i.e., $25^\circ$ west of south) at $600$ km/hr. Present the component form of the velocity of the airplane, taking the positive $x$-axis to represent due east and the positive $y$-axis due north.

A vector of magnitude $1$ is called a \defn{unit vector}.

The \defn{standard unit vectors} in space are $\i=\inner{1,0,0}$, $\j=\inner{0,1,0}$, and $\k=\inner{0,0,1}$. In the plane, the standard unit vectors are $\i=\inner{1,0}$ and $\j=\inner{0,1}$.

A \defn{linear combination} of vectors \textbf{v\textsubscript1}, \textbf{v\textsubscript2}, \dots, \textbf{v\textsubscript n} is an expression of the form

c_1\text{\textbf{v\textsubscript1}}+c_2\text{\textbf{v\textsubscript2}}+\cdots+c_n\text{\textbf{v\textsubscript n}},
where $c_1$, $c_2$, $\dots$, $c_n$ are scalars.

Any vector $\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ can be written as a linear combination of the standard unit vectors
$\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}=a_1\i+a_2\j+a_3\k$.
The components $a_1$, $a_2$, and $a_3$ are called the \defn{i-component}, \defn{j-component}, and \defn{k-component}, respectively, of $\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$.
Let $\a=10\i+2\j$, $\b=\i+\j$, and $\c=\i-\j$ be vectors in the plane. Present $\bm a$ as a linear combination of $\bm b$ and $\bm c$.
Present the unit vector parallel to the nonzero vector $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$.

The unit vector in the direction of $\a$ is called the \defn{direction} of $\a$.

A $75$-N weight is suspended by two wires, one that makes a $55^\circ$ angle from the ceiling and the other a $40^\circ$ angle. Present the force acting in each of the wires as a product of its length and direction.
An airplane flying due west at 500 mph in still air encounters a 70-mph tailwind blowing in the direction 60$^\circ$ north of west and an updraft of 20 mph. Present the ground speed and bearing of the airplane.

A vector $\a$ in $\R^n$ is denoted $\a=\inner{a_1,\,a_2,\,\dots,\,a_n}$, where the real numbers $a_1$, $a_2$, $\dots$, $a_n$ are called the components of $\a$.

The sum of $\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}$ and $\inner{b_1,b_2,\dots,b_n}$ is defined as

$\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}+\inner{b_1,b_2,\dots,b_n}=\inner{a_1+b_1,\,a_2+b_2,\,\dots,\,a_n+b_n}$.
If $k\in\R$ is a scalar, then the scalar multiple of $k$ and $\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}$ is defined as
$k\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}=\inner{ka_1,\,ka_2,\,\dots,ka_n}$.

Any vector $\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}$ can be written as a linear combination

$\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}=a_1\textbf e_1+a_2\textbf e_2+\cdots+a_n\textbf e_n$
of the standard unit vectors $\textbf e_1$, $\textbf e_2$, $\dots$, $\textbf e_n$, where
$\textbf e_i=\inner{e_{i1},e_{i2},\dots,e_{in}}$,
with $e_{ii}=1$ and $e_{ij}=0$ for $j\ne i$ for $i=1,2,\dots,n$.

The magnitude of $\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}$ is \[\abs{\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}}=\sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+\cdots+a_n^2}.\]

The vectors $\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}$ and $\inner{b_1,b_2,\dots,b_n}$ are equal if and only if their corresponding components are equal $a_1=b_1$, $a_2=b_2$, $\dots$, $a_n=b_n$.

The vectors $\a$, $\b$, $\c\in\R^n$ and scalars $r$, $s\in\R$ satisfy the following properties.
  1. $\a+\b=\b+\a$
  2. $(\a+\b)+\c=\a+(\b+\c)$
  3. $\a+\bm 0=\a$
  4. $\a+-\a=\bm 0$
  5. $1\a=\a$
  6. $r(s\a)=(rs)\a$
  7. $r(\a+\b)=r\a+r\b$
  8. $(r+s)\a=r\a+s\a$,
  9. $0\a=\bm 0$
