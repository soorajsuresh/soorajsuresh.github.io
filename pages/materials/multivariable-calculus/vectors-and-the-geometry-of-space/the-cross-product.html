Before continuing our discussion of vectors, we take a quick detour to acquaint ourselves with determinants. Otherwise, they will attend our discussion uninvited.

For real numbers $a$, $b$, $c$, and $d$, the collection of symbols

$\begin{vmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{vmatrix}$
is called a determinant. More specifically, it is called a 2x2 (read “2 by 2”) determinant, as it has 2 rows and 2 columns. This symbol represents the number $ad-bc$, the difference of the product of the numbers along the main diagonal (top-left to bottom-right) and the product of the numbers along the non-main diagonal (top-right to bottom-left), so
$\begin{vmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{vmatrix}=ad-bc$.

Compute $\begin{vmatrix}8&3\\2&2\end{vmatrix} $, $\begin{vmatrix}4&0\\-3&-1\end{vmatrix} $, and $\begin{vmatrix}1&2\\1&2\end{vmatrix}$.

For the real numbers $a_1$, $a_2$, $a_3$, $b_1$, $b_2$, $b_3$, $c_1$, $c_2$, and $c_3$, the collection of symbols

$\begin{vmatrix}a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\\c_1&c_2&c_3\end{vmatrix}$
is called a 3x3 determinant. It represents the number
$a_1b_2c_3-a_1b_3c_2-a_2b_1c_3+a_2b_3c_1+a_3b_1c_2-a_3b_2c_1$.

Note, the 3x3 determinant

$ \begin{vmatrix}a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\\c_1&c_2&c_3\end{vmatrix}$
is equal to the sum involving three 2x2 determinants
$ a_1\begin{vmatrix}b_2&b_3\\c_2&c_3\end{vmatrix}-a_2\begin{vmatrix}b_1&b_3\\c_1&c_3\end{vmatrix}+a_3\begin{vmatrix}b_1&b_2\\c_1&c_2\end{vmatrix} $,
which is $a_1$ multiplied by the 2x2 determinant obtained by removing the numbers from the row and column of $a_1$ from the 3x3 determinant minus $a_2$ times the 2x2 determinant obtained by removing the numbers from the row and column of $a_2$ from the 3x3 determinant plus $a_3$ times the 2x2 determinant obtained by removing the numbers from the row and column of $a_3$ from the 3x3 determinant.

Compute $\begin{vmatrix}1&2&-1\\3&0&1\\-5&4&2\end{vmatrix}$ and $\begin{vmatrix}\i&\j&\k\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{vmatrix}$.

The word normal is used interchangeably with the word perpendicular.

Let $\a$ and $\b$ be two nonzero vectors in space that are not parallel, and let $\theta$ be the angle between $\a$ and $\b$. Since they are not parallel, $\a$ and $\b$ determine a plane. There are two unit vectors normal to this plane. Let $\n$ be the unit normal vector in the direction determined by the right-hand rule: place the base of the right hand along $\a$, curl the fingers towards $\b$, and $\n$ points in the direction of the thumb.

The vector $(\abs\a\abs\b\sin\theta)\n$ is called the cross product of $\a$ and $\b$ and is denoted by $\a\times\b$ which is read “$\a$ cross $\b$”. If $\a$ and $\b$ are parallel or if one of $\a$ and $\b$ is $\bm0$, then we define $\a\times\b$ to be $\bm0$.
The cross product $\a\times\b$ is orthogonal to both $\a$ and $\b$.
The magnitude of $\a\times\b$ is
$\abs{\a\times\b}=\abs\a\abs\b\sin\theta.$
Nonzero vectors $\a$ and $\b$ are parallel if and only if $\a\times\b=\bm0$.
The magnitude of $\a\times\b$ is the area of the parallelogram determined by $\a$ and $\b$.
The cross product is not commutative.
If $\a$, $\b$, and $\c$ are vectors in space, and $r$ and $s$ are scalars, then:
  1. $\b\times\a=-(\a\times\b)$
  2. $(r\a)\times(s\b)=(rs)(\a\times\b)$
  3. $\a\times(\b+\c)=\a\times\b+\a\times\c$
  4. $(\b+\c)\times\a=\b\times\a+\c\times\a$
  5. $\a\times\a=\bm0$
  6. $\bm0\times\a=\bm0$

Note, $\i\times\j=\k$, $\j\times\k=\i$, and $\k\times\i=\j$.

The cross product is not associative.
If $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ and $\b=\inner{b_1,b_2,b_3}$, then compute $\a\times\b$.

So, if $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ and $\b=\inner{b_1,b_2,b_3}$, then

$\a\times\b=\begin{vmatrix}\i&\j&\k\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{vmatrix}$.

When a force $\F$ is applied to a wrench to turn a bolt, a torque is produced that causes the rotation of the bolt. The torque vector points in the direction of the axis of the bolt according to the right-hand rule (so the rotation is counterclockwise when viewed from the tip of the vector or clockwise when viewed from the tail of the vector). The magnitude of the torque is measured as $\abs{\textbf{\text{r}}}\abs{\F}\sin\theta$, where r represents the lever arm, and $\theta$ is the angle between r and $\F$. If $\n$ is a unit vector in the direction of the torque vector, then the torque vector is $(\abs{\textbf{\text{r}}}\abs{\F}\sin\theta)\n={\textbf{\text{r}}}\times\F$.

If $\a$, $\b$, and $\c$ are vectors in space, then which of the following expressions make sense?
  1. $\a\times(\b\times\c)$
  2. $\a\times(\b\cdot\c)$
  3. $(\a\times\b)\cdot\c$
  4. $(\b\cdot\c)\cdot\a$
  5. $(\b\cdot\c)\a$
  6. mosquito $\times$ mountain climber
What do the vertical bars mean in each expression?
  1. $\abs{\cos\theta}$
  2. $\begin{vmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{vmatrix}$
  3. $\abs{\a}$
  4. $\abs{\a\times\b}$
  5. $\abs{\a\cdot\b}$
  6. $\abs{(\a\times\b)\cdot\c}$

The product $(\a\times\b)\cdot\c$ is called the scalar triple product of $\a$, $\b$, and $\c$.

Note, $\abs{(\a\times\b)\cdot\c}$ is the volume of the parallelepiped (parallelogram-sided box) determined by $\a$, $\b$, and $\c$ (where $\a$ and $\b$ determine the base), so it is also called the box product of $\a$, $\b$, and $\c$.

Note, $(\a\times\b)\cdot\c=(\c\times\a)\cdot\b=(\b\times\c)\cdot\a$, and $(\a\times\b)\cdot\c=\a\cdot(\b\times\c)$.
