The set of all real numbers is denoted by $\R$. If $S$ is a set, then we write $x\in S$ to denote that $x$ is an element of $S$. For example, $\sqrt2\in\R$.

The plane is the set $\R^2$ of all ordered pairs $(x,y)$, where $x,y\in\R$.

Space is the set $\R^3$ of all ordered triplets $(x,y,z)$, where $x,y,z\in\R$.

To represent space graphically, three mutually perpendicular lines called the coordinate axes that meet at the point $(0,0,0)$ called the origin are arranged to form a right-handed coordinate frame such that when you place the base of your right hand on the positive $x$-axis and curl your fingers towards the positive $y$-axis, the direction of the $z$-axis is parallel with the direction of your thumb.

The rectangular coordinates $x$, $y$, and $z$ of a point $(x,y,z)$ represent distances along the $x$-, $y$-, and $z$-axes.

The plane formed by the $x$- and $y$- axes is the $xy$-plane. The plane formed by $x$- and $z$- axes is the $xz$-plane. The plane formed by the $y$- and $z$- axes is the $yz$-plane. These three planes are called the coordinate planes, and they cut space into eight octants. The first octant

is
$O_1=\build{(x,y,z)}{x\ge0,\, y\ge0,\, z\ge0}$.

If $P$ is a plane containing a curve $C$ and $L$ is a line not parallel to $P$, then the set of all lines through $C$ that are parallel to $L$ is called a cylinder with directrix $C$ and generatrix $L$.

A sphere is the set of all points a distance $\rho>0$ away from a point $C$ in space; the distance $\rho$ is called the radius of the sphere, and the point $C$ is called the center of the sphere.

The coordinates $x$, $y$, and $z$ of any point $(x,y,z)$ on the sphere with radius $\rho>0$ and center $C=(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ satisfy the standard equation

$\parens{x-x_0}^2+\parens{y-y_0}^2+\parens{z-z_0}^2=\rho^2$.

A ball with radius $\rho$ and center $C$ is the set of all points on and interior to the sphere of radius $\rho$ and center $C$. A hemisphere is half of a sphere.
