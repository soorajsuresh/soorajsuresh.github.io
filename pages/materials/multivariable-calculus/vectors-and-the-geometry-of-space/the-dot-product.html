A displacement is a directed distance, i.e., a vector representing the shortest path from one point to another, e.g., $\overrightarrow{AB}$.

In [previous section], if a force vector $\F$ was applied to a particle at an angle of $\theta$ radians from the horizontal, then we expressed $\F$ in component form as

$\F=\inner{\abs\F\cos\theta,\abs\F\sin\theta}$.
The number $\abs\F\cos\theta$ represents the component of $\F$ effective in moving the particle in the horizontal direction, and $\abs\F\sin\theta$ represents the component of $\F$ effective in moving the particle in the vertical direction.

It will be necessary to know the components of $\F$ effective in moving the particle in other directions as well. Specifically, if $\F$ moves a particle through a displacement $\D$, then we will need to know the component of $\F$ effective in moving the particle through $\D$ (where $\D$ may not be parallel to a coordinate axis).

The component of $\F$ effective in moving the particle through $\D$ is $\abs\F\cos\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\F$ and $\D$. Our first objective is to find an efficient way to compute the angle between two vectors.

When two nonzero vectors $\a$ and $\b$ are arranged such that their tails coincide, they form an angle $\theta$ measured between $0$ and $\pi$ radians in the plane containing $\a$ and $\b$. This angle $\theta$ is called the angle between $\a$ and $\b$. If $\a$ and $\b$ have the same direction, then $\theta=0$, and if $\a$ and $\b$ are opposite in direction, then $\theta=\pi$.
Determine the angle $\theta$ between the two nonzero vectors $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ and $\b=\inner{b_1,b_2,b_3}$.
If $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,a_3}$ and $\b=\inner{b_1,b_2,b_3}$, then the quantity $a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3$ is denoted by $\a\cdot\b$, read “$\a$ dot $\b$”, and called the dot product of $\a$ and $\b$:
$ \a\cdot\b=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3 $.

With this notation, the angle between two nonzero vectors $\a$ and $\b$ in space is

$ \theta=\acos\parens{\frac{\a\cdot\b}{\abs\a\abs\b}} $.

Note, this operation between two vectors results in a scalar.

Find $\a\cdot\b$ if $\a=\inner{2,0,-2}$, $\b=\inner{1,1,3}$.
A water main is to be constructed with a 10% grade in the north direction and a 20% grade in the east direction. Determine the angle required in the water main for the turn from north to east.

By the first example, we have the following theorem.

If $\a$ and $\b$ are nonzero vectors in space and $\theta$ is the angle between them, then
$ \a\cdot\b=\abs\a\abs\b\cos\theta $.

This theorem allows us to easily compute the dot product of two vectors when their magnitudes and the angle between them are more readily accessible than their components.

Recall that the component of the force $\F$ effective in moving a particle through a displacement $\D$ is $\abs\F\cos\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\F$ and $\D$.

The number $\abs\F\cos\theta$ is called the scalar projection of $\F$ onto $\D$ and is denoted $\scal_\D\F$. The vector with this magnitude in the direction of $\D$ is called the vector projection of $\F$ onto $\D$ and is denoted $\proj_\D\F$.

Note that $\scal_\D\F$ is negative when $\frac\pi2<\theta\le\pi$.

In integral calculus, the work done by a constant force $\F$ applied in the direction of the displacement $\D$ to move the particle through $\D$ was $W=\abs\F\abs\D$. (In integral calculus, we did not need vectors, and we just wrote $W=FD$. Here, we have rewritten that idea using vector notation to relate our old idea of work to the more general definition of work below.)

If a constant force $\F$ is applied in a direction different from the direction of the displacement $\D$ to move a particle through $\D$, then work is not done by the full magnitude of the force, but only by the component of $\F$ that is in the direction of $\D$, so

$W=(\scal_\D\F)\abs\D$.

Note, $W=(\scal_\D\F)\abs\D=(\abs\F\cos\theta)\abs\D=\abs\F\abs\D\cos\theta=\F\cdot\D$.

The work done by a constant force $\F$ moving a particle through a displacement $\D$ is
$W=\F\cdot\D$.

Furthermore, $\a\cdot\b>0$ if and only if $\theta$ is acute, and $\a\cdot\b<0$ if and only if $\theta$ is obtuse.

The dot product can be defined between two vectors in $\R^n$.

If $\a=\inner{a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n}$ and $\b=\inner{b_1,b_2,\dots,b_n}$, then

$ \a\cdot\b=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+\cdots+a_nb_n $

All the properties and definitions involving the dot product that we have discussed hold for this general definition. We will mostly apply the dot product in space ($\R^3$) and in the plane ($\R^2$).

If $\a,\b,\c\in\R^n$, then prove that $\a\cdot(\b+\c)=\a\cdot\b+\a\cdot\c$.
