Curvature and Normal Vectors of a Curve

As a particle moves along a smooth curve, the vector $\T=\ddx[\r][s]$ turns.

Recall, $\T$ is a unit vector, so its length is constant, so only its direction changes. The rate at which the unit tangent vector $\T$ of a smooth curve $C$ turns per unit length along $C$ (i.e., with respect to arc length $s$ along $C$) is called curvature, denoted $\kappa$.

$ \kappa=\abs{\ddx[\T][s]}. $

If $C$ is parametrized by $\r(t)$ where $t$ is some parameter different from arc length $s$,

$ \kappa=\abs{\ddx[\T][s]}=\abs{\ddx[\T][t]\ddx[t][s]}=\abs{\ddx[t][s]}\abs{\ddx[\T][t]}=\frac1{\abs{\ddx[s][t]}}\abs{\ddx[\T][t]}=\frac1{\abs\v}\abs{\ddx[\T][t]}. $
$ \kappa=\frac1{\abs\v}\abs{\ddx[\T][t]}. $

Present the curvature for a circle (in the plane).

Again, $\T$ is a unit vector, so it has constant length. By our very important observation, $\ddx[\T][s]$ is orthogonal to $\T$. Note, $\ddx[\T][s]$ is not necessarily a unit vector itself. A unit vector orthogonal to $\T$ is

$ \frac1{\abs{\ddx[\T][s]}}\ddx[\T][s]=\frac1\kappa\ddx[\T][s]. $
$ \N=\frac1\kappa\ddx[\T][s]. $

The vector $\ddx[\T][s]$ always points in the direction in which $\T$ turns, and $\N$ is the unit vector in the direction of $\ddx[\T][s]$, so $\N$ always points in the direction in which $\T$ turns.

We may want to compute $\N$ without reparametrizing $C$ in terms of arc length and without computing $\kappa$. If $C$ is parametrized by $\r(t)$ where $t$ is some parameter different from arc length $s$, then

$ \N=\frac{\ddx[\T][t]}{\abs{\ddx[\T][t]}}. $
$ \N=\frac{\ddx[\T][t]}{\abs{\ddx[\T][t]}}. $

The circle of curvature or osculating circle at a point $P$ on a plane curve where $\kappa\ne0$ is the circle in the plane of the curve that

  1. is tangent to the curve at $P$,
  2. has the same curvature $\kappa$ that the curve has at $P$ (and hence has radius $1/\kappa$), and
  3. has center $Q$ such that $\overrightarrow{PQ}$ is parallel to $\N$.
